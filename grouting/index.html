The clay plains north of Dallas don’t forgive guesswork. In Plano, where the Taylor Marl meets pockets of alluvium from the Trinity River tributaries, designing a grouting program starts long before the first hose is connected. The local geology, shaped by Cretaceous sedimentary deposits and a seasonal rainfall pattern that delivers over 40 inches per year, creates swelling pressures that can overwhelm a poorly conceived injection plan. We approach every project by cross-referencing grain-size curves with Atterberg data to determine whether a cement-bentonite or ultrafine cement mix will actually penetrate the formation, and we often pair that with in-situ permeability testing when the target zone is a silty sand lens that refuses standard grout uptake. The goal isn’t just to fill voids; it’s to design a grout that travels where it’s needed, sets without excessive bleed, and holds under the fluctuating groundwater table that characterizes Collin County’s spring and fall cycles.

A Plano grouting design lives or dies by the Marsh funnel: if viscosity exceeds 50 seconds, the grout won’t reach the target horizon before gelling.

Our approach and scope

We recently completed a design for a commercial building near Legacy West where the exploratory borings showed a 6-foot layer of desiccated, fractured clay overlying a loose sand pocket at 18 feet. The contractor had already budgeted for permeation grouting, but the real question was whether to use microfine cement or a sodium silicate solution given the target permeability of 10⁻³ cm/s. Our lab ran a full ASTM D2487 soil classification on undisturbed Shelby tube samples, then designed a grout mix with a Marsh viscosity under 45 seconds and a 24-hour unconfined compressive strength above 150 psi. The injection pressure profile was staggered: 40 psi at the top of the sand layer, dropping to 25 psi near the clay interface to avoid hydrofracture. In Plano’s urban infill sites, where adjacent structures are often less than 15 feet away, that kind of pressure control is not optional. We also verified the set time against ambient soil temperatures recorded at 68°F, which is typical for the local water table depth during winter construction.

Regional considerations

In Plano, the most common failure we see isn’t equipment malfunction; it’s hydraulic jacking of the pavement because nobody accounted for the confining stress of a desiccated crust. When the surface clay is dry and stiff, injecting at pressures that would be safe in saturated soil can lift a slab faster than you can shut the valve. Another recurring issue is grout loss into unsealed utility trenches: the city’s older neighborhoods along 15th Street and Parker Road have storm drains and fiber optic conduits that create preferential flow paths, and if the design doesn’t include a staged grouting sequence with a blocking perimeter, half the material ends up in a manhole. We also watch for sulfate-rich groundwater, which is present in isolated pockets of the Eagle Ford formation and can degrade ordinary Portland cement grouts over time unless a sulfate-resistant mix is specified from the start.

Common questions

How much does a grouting design package cost for a Plano project?

Our design fees for Plano projects range from US$1,170 to US$3,760 depending on the number of injection zones, the complexity of the geological profile, and the number of lab mixes that need to be formulated. A typical residential underpinning design falls on the lower end, while a multi-row curtain grouting plan for a commercial basement with QA/QC field monitoring approaches the upper end.

How do you determine the right grout type for Plano’s expansive clay?

We start with a full geotechnical index test suite, Atterberg limits and hydrometer analysis, to classify the clay per ASTM D2487. If the activity ratio is high and fractures are open, we lean toward a low-viscosity microfine cement that can penetrate fissures without desiccating the clay further. For compaction grouting, we design a stiff, low-slump mix that displaces rather than permeates, which works better in the plastic Taylor Marl.

What injection pressures should we expect in Plano soils?

It varies by depth and soil type. In the sandy alluvium lenses we find between 15 and 25 feet, permeation grouting pressures typically run 25 to 40 psi. In the overlying stiff clay, we limit pressure to under 30 psi to avoid hydrofracture. Our designs include a pressure-versus-depth envelope that accounts for the overburden stress and the tensile strength of the formation.

Do you handle the field execution or just the design?

We provide the complete design package including mix formulations, injection sequences, and QA/QC protocols. While we don’t self-perform the grouting, we stay on site during the first injection rounds to verify that pressures, volumes, and set times match the design assumptions. If the ground response differs from the model, we adjust the mix or staging in real time.
